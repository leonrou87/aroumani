I get asked versions of this question a lot: "What does an engineering director actually do?"

The honest answer takes longer than most people want.

## The Trap

Early in my career — as a developer at BlackBerry, then as a manager at Amazon — I made the classic mistake. I optimized for technical excellence. The code was good. The architecture was clean. The team shipped reliably.

But I was measuring the wrong thing.

Technical excellence is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one. It's table stakes. The engineering leaders I admire most aren't known for their system design — they're known for the teams they built, the problems they identified before anyone else did, and the culture they created where their best people stayed and grew.

## What It's Actually About

**Trust over process.** The instinct when you scale a team is to add process — more reviews, more sign-offs, more checkpoints. Some process is necessary. But the most productive engineering cultures I've worked in were built on trust, not process. Trust that your engineers will escalate the right things. Trust that they're making thoughtful decisions. Trust that's earned, not assumed — but genuine when extended.

**Clarity as a leadership act.** The hardest part of my job is making sure everyone on my team knows what matters and why. Not the roadmap — the *reason* behind the roadmap. When engineers understand the strategic context, they make better decisions autonomously. That saves me from becoming a bottleneck. It also makes the work more meaningful.

**Protecting the environment.** The job of an engineering leader is, in part, to create the conditions for great work. That means running interference with organizational complexity so your team can focus. It means being the one who has the hard conversations early so problems don't fester. It means caring about things like physical space, meeting load, and team morale — not as nice-to-haves but as engineering inputs.

**Developing people is the work.** The most leveraged thing I do is invest in the growth of the people around me. A senior engineer who develops into a strong technical lead multiplies the team's capacity for years. Identifying that potential, giving the right stretch opportunity, providing real feedback — that's not a side responsibility. It's the main one.

## The Uber Context

At Uber, leading the Mobility Verticals Group means sitting at the intersection of transportation, technology, and global scale. The decisions my teams make affect how people in Seattle, Cape Town, Paris, and dozens of other cities get around.

That's not lost on me. And I try not to let it be lost on my teams.

When people are connected to the meaning of their work — not just the ticket, not just the sprint, but the actual human impact — they do their best work. My job is to maintain that connection, especially as the organization grows and the distance between an engineer and a rider increases.

---

Engineering leadership is mostly a human problem dressed up as a technical one. The sooner you accept that, the better you'll get at it.
